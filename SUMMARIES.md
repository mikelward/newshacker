# SUMMARIES.md — AI summary card layout

The thread page renders two AI summary cards above the comments list:
an article summary (`SummaryCard`, `src/components/Thread.tsx:207`)
and a comments summary (`CommentsSummaryCard`,
`src/components/Thread.tsx:296`). Both are generated by Gemini and
fetched asynchronously, which creates the core layout problem this
doc exists to document.

## The problem

When the real summary arrives, the card is drawn. If the card's
height changes at that moment, every comment below jumps. Mid-read,
that is disruptive — the user loses their place and the perceived
quality drops sharply. The inverse failure, reserving much more
height than the real summary needs, leaves an ugly empty band at the
bottom of the card.

We want:

- **No jump when the summary arrives.** A small amount of steady empty
  space is acceptable; a sudden shove of the comments downward is not.
- **No extra tap target to reveal the summary.** An "Expand" button
  would trivially solve the jump problem, but it would violate the
  fewer-larger-tap-targets rule in `SPEC.md` (§ "Primary design
  problem") — the thread page already has the overflow-menu button
  and the article link, and a third optional tappable on the summary
  card would erode the same UX budget the row layout is carefully
  protecting. The summary auto-loads and is sized to fit in place.
- **Correct on cold, cache-warmed, and offline paths.** The card must
  render at a sensible size with no summary text available yet, and
  must not regress when service-worker cache returns the text
  synchronously.

## Current approach

The card uses a skeleton-first render with a reserved height that
stays in place when the real text arrives. Four coordinated pieces
(`d52f9a6 Stabilize AI summary card heights during load`):

1. **Measured card width, not assumed.** Each card holds a ref and
   reads `clientWidth` through `useContentWidth`
   (`src/hooks/useContentWidth.ts`, ResizeObserver-based). This keeps
   the skeleton sized to whatever the actual card is, including
   tablet breakpoints and dynamic viewport changes.
2. **Canvas-measured line count.** `estimateWrappedLines`
   (`src/lib/skeletonSize.ts`) measures the summary font's average
   char width via a `<canvas>` text-metrics pass, applies a 0.9
   word-wrap efficiency factor, and returns the number of lines the
   reserved character budget will wrap to at the measured width.
3. **`min-height` floor on the card.** Once the width is known, the
   card's computed minimum height is pinned to the skeleton's
   loading-state height. A shorter-than-expected real summary renders
   inside that space; the card never shrinks on arrival.
4. **`overflow-anchor: none`.** Any residual content-height change
   therefore pushes the off-screen content below the viewport
   downward, rather than shoving the user's reading position upward.

The skeleton stays on screen until the real text arrives and is then
replaced in place. The `aria-live="polite"` / `aria-busy` pair on
each card announces the state change to assistive tech without
interrupting.

## Alternatives considered

A few shapes of this feature were considered and rejected before
settling on the skeleton-with-reservation approach above. Kept here
so a later contributor can reconsider without re-losing the
reasoning.

### Server-side prefetch of summaries

Run a background job that polls HN's top-stories feed and calls
Gemini on each new story at ingest, warming the `/api/summary` cache
before any reader opens the story — making time-to-summary close to
zero for the first reader of each popular item.

- **Why not now.** `AGENTS.md` rule 6 caps the architecture at
  "HN's API + stateless Vercel functions" — no persistent backend
  service, no database, no workers. A poller turns the project
  into a stateful system with its own uptime, its own rate-limit
  relationship with HN, and its own reconciliation concerns.
- **Current behavior already handles the common case.** Generated
  summaries are shared across readers via Vercel's edge CDN (see
  `8c3b21b Share AI summary caches across all servers via Vercel
  edge CDN`) and locally via the PWA service worker. Only the
  first reader of a given story waits on Gemini; every subsequent
  reader hits cache in tens of milliseconds. At single-digit daily
  traffic the cold-first-reader case is not a perceived problem.
- **What it would cost.** A Vercel cron polling `topstories.json`
  every few minutes is within the free plan's cron allowance, but
  each prefetched story is a Gemini call whether or not any reader
  opens it. Top 30 stories × ~24 polls/day ≈ hundreds of Gemini
  calls against stories no one touches — multiplying our Gemini
  spend for a marginal latency gain.
- **When to reconsider.** When the cold-generation wait on the
  first reader of a fresh story becomes a perceived quality
  problem. Concrete signals: (a) consistently high time-to-summary
  on stories with a low `score`, since those are fresh and
  unlikely to be cache-warm; (b) a material share of sessions
  where the summary is still loading when the reader taps back to
  the feed; (c) Gemini pricing where always-on prefetch is cheaper
  than our current on-demand model. Any one of those makes the
  no-backend rule worth revisiting.

### Block thread-page render until summaries are ready

Wait on the server (or in the client's loader) for both Gemini
responses before painting the thread page at all. No skeleton, no
reservation, no reflow — the page appears once with the real
summary already in place.

- **Why not now.** It introduces a hard latency floor on the
  thread page equal to the slower of the two Gemini calls —
  measured p95 around a second for the article summary, up to
  several seconds for the comments summary. That is an
  unacceptable regression for the common case (a returning reader
  whose cache is warm and who would have seen the thread in tens
  of milliseconds), imposed to avoid a reflow that only affects
  the cold case.
- **When to reconsider.** Never for the whole page; possibly for
  the summary cards in isolation if Gemini latency drops by an
  order of magnitude (sub-100ms p95) and the service worker can
  serve the summary inside the same request as the item fetch.
  At that point "wait for it" is indistinguishable from "render
  it immediately" and the reservation can be dropped.

### Rely on `overflow-anchor: none` (CSS jump prevention) alone

The card is already marked `overflow-anchor: none` so the browser
anchors scroll to a following comment when content changes. One
could argue that alone solves the reading-position problem and the
reservation is unnecessary.

- **Why it is not enough.** Scroll anchoring only hides movement
  that happens **off-screen**. When the reader is actively looking
  at the summary card — which is the common case on a cold open,
  because the summary card is the first thing on the page — the
  card visibly growing by a line or two on arrival is itself the
  distraction, even with their scroll position preserved. The
  reservation pattern prevents the visible growth; scroll
  anchoring only prevents the secondary effect of losing the
  reader's place when the growth happens below the fold.
  Anchoring is kept as defense-in-depth for any residual drift
  the reservation misses, not as a standalone strategy.
- **When to reconsider.** If Gemini ever returns summaries
  synchronously within the initial server response (e.g. an
  edge function that inlines both the item data and the summary)
  the reader never sees the skeleton → real transition at all,
  and both the reservation and the anchoring become dead
  complexity. That is the same "summaries are effectively free
  and instant" world as the row above.

### Client-side summarization in the browser

Generate the summary on the client from the article HTML, via a
WebAssembly model or a browser-native summarizer API (e.g. Chrome's
`window.ai.summarizer`).

- **Why not now.** Current browser summarizers are either too
  heavy (WASM models blow the Lighthouse bundle target in
  `SPEC.md` § "Performance Targets") or not yet broadly shipped
  (on-device APIs are experimental and Chromium-only). Moving
  generation to the client also loses the shared edge-cached
  benefit — each reader of each story would pay the generation
  cost from scratch rather than hitting the CDN.
- **When to reconsider.** When a browser-native summarizer ships
  to stable across major browsers at zero download cost, it
  becomes a useful fallback for offline or Gemini-is-down cases
  without a new dependency.

## The tunable constants

Three numbers in `src/components/Thread.tsx` drive how much vertical
space each card reserves. All three are hand-tuned from device
measurements, prompt benchmarks, and observed Gemini output.

- **`ARTICLE_SUMMARY_EXPECTED_CHARS`** (`Thread.tsx:168`) — currently
  `230`. The Gemini prompt asks for a single concise sentence;
  on-device measurement (Pixel 10, Lenovo tablet) showed real
  summaries clustering at 100–210 chars with a long tail to ~240.
  Reserving for 230 covers the long tail on narrow mobile widths
  without growing on arrival; on tablet widths it still resolves to
  roughly three lines.

- **`INSIGHT_EXPECTED_CHARS`** (`Thread.tsx:177`) — currently `75`.
  The comments-summary prompt caps each insight at 13 words (the
  WORDS13 variant from
  `scripts/benchmark-comments-prompt.mjs`; winner of the 2026-04 prompt
  bench on max-chars × p95 latency). Measured Flash-Lite output under
  that cap maxed at 71 chars across a 10-story benchmark
  (`833e15c Tighten comment-insight char budget from 85 to 75`). `75`
  gives a 4-char cushion over observed max while rounding down to one
  line per insight on tablet (≥640px card width) — the previous `85`
  sat right at the tablet round-up edge and left three or four empty
  rows at the bottom of the card. Phone (≤~400px) still resolves to
  two lines, unchanged.

- **`EXPECTED_INSIGHT_COUNT`** (`Thread.tsx:183`) — currently `5`. The
  prompt asks for up to 5 insights (fewer if the discussion is thin,
  explicitly "3 or 4 is fine"). Reserving for the max means rich
  threads render with zero whitespace; thin threads keep the card's
  loading-state height with blank space at the bottom, consistent
  with the strict no-reflow rule.

## Known risk

If Gemini ever produces output exceeding the reserved budgets — an
article summary beyond ~240 chars on a narrow phone, or an insight
over ~80 chars on tablet — the affected card will grow one line on
arrival. Observed maxes (Pixel 10 + Lenovo tablet, ~10-story benchmark
per constant) sat comfortably under the budget, so this is a tail
risk. If it ever shows up in the field, the fix is to bump the
relevant `EXPECTED_CHARS` constant and update the nearby comment with
the new measurement cohort.
